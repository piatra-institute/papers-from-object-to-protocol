# Review: from-object-to-protocol

## Verdict
**Accept with major revisions, leaning reject in its current form.** The five-property "protocol" frame and the latent-score-vs-static-AI distinction are genuinely useful analytic tools, and the prose is controlled and largely alive. But the paper's actual novel contribution — the "third migration" into *latent score art* — is grounded on **zero verified real-world instances**: its only concrete examples are the institute's own (apparently not-yet-existing) "Prism" kits and an explicitly hypothetical "music protocol." A genealogy that traces sixty years of real, named, conserved works and then lands its headline category on a self-promotional, unrealized example is asserting the very thing it claims to observe. That, not the (real) citation slips, is the most serious problem.

## Claim grounding

- "Contemporary AI art completes the migration: the work increasingly resides in an executable package…" (Abstract) → **Overstated / unsupported.** "Completes" and "increasingly resides" assert a trend the paper never evidences with a single existing artwork or market datum. The only instances given are the institute's own kit and a hypothetical.
- "The Piatra Prism project… is one practical site for this category. The deliverable… is the *world-generation kit*…" (§6) → **Unsupported and non-independent.** Stated in the present tense but reads as a description of intended practice, not an observed work. Using the institute's own studio as the lead example of the headline category is a conflict that the standalone-artifact requirement cannot absorb.
- "A second example: a music protocol might specify a prompt grammar… Each rendered track is a realization." (§6) → **Explicitly hypothetical** ("might specify"). The two examples carrying the third migration are one self-reference and one conditional.
- "the same script and seed produce the same image" (§5) → **Slightly overstated.** Art Blocks' deterministic guarantee is at the hash-seed level; rendered pixels can vary with browser/GPU. True enough for the argument, but the categorical phrasing overshoots.
- "The U.S. Copyright Office (2023) has held that prompts alone… do not provide sufficient creative control to ground copyright." (§8.1) → **Mildly anachronistic.** The sharp "prompts alone are insufficient" position is articulated most fully in the Office's **2025** Part 2 report; the 2023 guidance is primarily a disclosure rule (built on *Zarya of the Dawn*). Defensible as a gloss, but it back-dates a later, firmer holding.
- "the modern object-centered configuration is the deviation from a longer rule… The object configuration was the exception. The protocol is the longer rule." (§2, §10) → **Overstated by equivocation.** See Argument below: the deep-past "specification" (function/canon/rite) is a different kind of thing from a generative instruction set, and the paper itself concedes pre-object regimes "did not always hold explicit transferability or formal editioning."
- "This is medium-specific criticism, in the line of Greenberg, Krauss, and Manovich…" (§8.3) → **Supported as lineage**, though it is the only place Krauss is used, and the substantive Greenbergian "medium specificity" claim is named, not argued.

## Argument

- **§2/§7/§10 — the load-bearing equivocation on "specification."** The thesis depends on treating a tomb relief's function-anchored identity, a workshop canon, a Cage score, an Art Blocks script, and an AI package as instances of one thing ("the protocol / the specification"). But "identity fixed by ritual function" is not "a rule-bound description of how to produce admissible realizations." A Byzantine icon is not *transferable*, *editioned*, or *realized-from-a-spec* in the §7 sense; the paper admits this in §7 ("did not always hold explicit transferability or formal editioning") — which quietly concedes that the properties defining a protocol are *absent* from the deep past it claims as the "longer rule." The genealogy's strongest rhetorical move ("the object was the exception") rests on the weakest link.
- **§7 — the definition is built to fit the examples.** The five properties are gradable, not binary, and are selected so that Cage/LeWitt/Art Blocks/AI all pass. There is no negative case worked through — no artwork shown *not* to be a protocol under the test. With gradable criteria and no counter-instance, the diagnostic risks being unfalsifiable: every conventional painting also follows inherited recipes, canons, and reproduction rights. The paper never confronts the "if everything is a protocol, the category does no work" objection.
- **§6 — the central analogy carries argumentative weight it cannot bear.** "Just as a score for piano cannot be performed identically on cello, a prompt-score for Stable Diffusion 1.5 cannot be performed identically on Midjourney v6." The disanalogy is unaddressed: a cellist *interprets* a determinate score; a diffusion model *statistically transforms* an underdetermined prompt. Calling the model a "designated instrument" and a LoRA a "rehearsal annotation" (the §6 table) is asserted as "one-to-one mapping," not defended.
- **§8.2 — the market section is speculation styled as analysis.** The six-rung "ladder of robustness" and "most viable latent score artworks will combine…" are presented with the confidence of findings but rest on no market data, no transaction, no case. The El País citation is the only external anchor and it is a single journalistic aside.
- **Missing alternative explanation.** The paper never seriously entertains that "object vs. specification" is a *matter of degree present in all art* rather than a migrating locus — which would dissolve the genealogy into a truism. Nor does it engage the obvious rival framing (its own "distributed-authorship" thesis from §1) as a genuine competitor; it is dismissed in one sentence as "an attribution problem."

**Genuinely strong:** the certificate reframing in §5 ("the script is the work, the chain is the certificate, and the realization is the rendered token") is crisp and earns its place. §9 (Opacity, Drift, Dead Platforms) is honest, specific, and resists the triumphalism the thesis invites — substrate drift and conservation-as-open-problem are real and well put. The latent-score-vs-static-AI distinction in §6/§7 is a usable categorical contribution even if over-extended into the deep past.

## Factual errors

- **LeWitt "Paragraphs on Conceptual Art" is 1967, not 1968** (verified). The volume/issue cited (5(10)) is itself the June 1967 issue, so the citation is internally inconsistent.
- **Weiner's 1968 text is the "Declaration of Intent," not "Statement of Intent"** (verified); "Statement of Intent" is a separate 1969 work.
- **Cohen/AARON "late 1960s"** is early; AARON is an early-1970s program (low–med confidence).
- The Vasari reference omits the 1550 edition the body cites. No other date, figure, or attribution checked was wrong: Fountain (1917), 4'33" (1952), Art Blocks (2020), fxhash (2021), 88 FR 16190 (2023), Young's *#10* dedication, *Grapefruit*'s publisher all verified correct.

## Prose and insight

**Alive:** the opening of §1 ("The painting hangs on the wall; the photograph rests in the print; the film is the cut, the song is the master") earns its cadence. §2's "A tomb image is not fully present without the tomb. A workshop altarpiece is not fully present without the altar" is genuinely good — concrete, escalating, doing conceptual work. The §5 certificate-dissolution passage is the intellectual high point. §9 is the most trustworthy writing in the paper because it argues against its own enthusiasm.

**Dead:** §8.2's "ladder of robustness" is a consulting deck in prose — "cheap, copyable, fragile… better, and the license has to define the substrate" — and adds no insight a bulleted product roadmap wouldn't. The §6 mapping table is a list asserting analogies rather than testing them. The conclusion (§10) re-walks the five-stage genealogy a third time; by then "the work climbed up to the score… moved further into the script… is the executable package" is incantation, not argument. The repeated "one layer up" tic (Abstract, §1, §4, §6, §10) wears thin.

**Genuine contribution:** a clean diagnostic vocabulary (substrate-relative / bounded / transferable / editioned / governed) for sorting specification-based from object-based work, plus the latent-score / static-AI distinction. That is real and reusable. The historical over-claim ("the protocol is the longer rule") is the part most likely to be cited and least likely to survive scrutiny.

## Top fixes

1. **Ground the third migration in at least one real, externally verifiable latent-score artwork** — named, by another maker, that a reader can inspect. Demote or clearly frame the Piatra Prism example as the institute's own program-in-progress, not evidence. Without an existing instance, the headline category is a proposal, and the paper should say so in the abstract.
2. **Fix the two citation errors:** LeWitt 1967 (not 1968), and Weiner "Declaration of Intent" (not "Statement of Intent"). Verify the El País reference against its actual URL/date or replace it; correct "late 1960s" for AARON.
3. **Resolve the "specification" equivocation in §2/§7.** Either argue explicitly that deep-past function-anchored images satisfy the five properties, or concede they don't and weaken "the protocol is the longer rule" to a claim about the conceptual-to-AI lineage only. The current text wants both and contradicts itself in §7.
4. **Add a negative case to §7.** Work through one artwork the diagnostic *excludes*, and address head-on the "if everything is a protocol, the category is vacuous" objection. A frame that admits no counter-instance is not yet doing analytic work.
5. **Either engage or cut the six uncited references** (Benjamin, Danto, Dickie, Gell, Strathern, Zylinska). Benjamin especially: a paper on editioning and the migration off the object must engage "aura," or stop listing him as though it had.
6. **Replace the §8.2 market speculation with either data or an explicit "speculative" flag.** The "ladder of robustness" presents conjecture as structure; one real transaction, platform policy, or priced edition would do more than the whole ranked list.
7. **Defend the §6 instrument/score analogy against its disanalogy** (interpretive performer vs. statistical transform), since the entire "substrate is part of the work" claim rests on it.
8. **Cut the third restatement of the genealogy in §10** and the recurring "one layer up" refrain; let §5 and §9 carry the weight.
