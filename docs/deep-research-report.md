# From Cave Wall to Latent Space

## Abstract and thesis options

### Abstract

This dossier argues that art is best understood not as a timeless category of “expression,” but as a historically shifting human technology for making relations visible, durable, authoritative, exchangeable, and executable. In prehistoric contexts, images likely served overlapping roles in ritual, cognition, memory, and group formation, even if motives remain evidentially underdetermined. In ancient states and empires, images became instruments of cosmology, funerary continuity, kingship, public theology, and political legitimacy. In medieval and early religious settings, art often functioned less as autonomous object than as sacred labor, devotional interface, and workshop practice. Renaissance patronage increasingly personalized the artist while still embedding production in contracts, courts, guilds, and ecclesiastical demands. Early modern markets made artworks portable commodities for bourgeois interiors and collecting. Romanticism and modernism elevated expression, authenticity, and rupture, but that “expressive” model was historically late, partial, and often mythologized. Twentieth-century conceptual and institutional art then displaced the unique object with declaration, context, certificate, score, or instruction. Generative and computational art further redistributed form-making to systems, code, randomness, and rule sets. AI art intensifies this trajectory by making artistic production depend on model architectures, training corpora, prompts, reference assets, seeds, adapters, interfaces, and platform governance. The strongest conclusion is not that AI abolishes authorship, but that it makes distributed authorship explicit. Contemporary AI art is therefore simultaneously a medium, an authorship regime, a market structure, and a participatory instruction system. The category “latent score art” is promising, but only for a narrow subset of AI works: cases in which the artwork’s identity lies chiefly in an executable package that specifies how a user or collector navigates a model-dependent possibility space. For the broader field, “AI art” remains the umbrella term; “latent score art” is more precise as a subcategory for collector-executed or protocol-based works. citeturn28view0turn28view1turn28view2turn28view3turn28view4turn28view5turn28view6turn29view2turn29view3turn29view4turn29view5turn29view7turn29view8turn22search3turn19search1turn19search4turn37view4turn29view11turn29view12turn30view6turn32view5turn30view3turn32view10

### Central thesis

The best thesis to carry into a finished paper is this: **AI art is neither a total rupture nor a simple continuation. It is a reconfiguration of older art functions in which medium, instruction, institution, market, and authorship become newly entangled through model-dependent generation and latent-space navigation.** citeturn19search1turn19search4turn37view4turn29view11turn29view12turn30view6turn32view5turn30view3

### Thesis options

- **Reconfiguration thesis**: AI art is best understood as a reconfiguration of older artistic functions rather than the birth of an unprecedented category. This is the strongest option because it explains both continuity with instruction-based and generative art and the genuine novelty of trained models and platform infrastructures. citeturn29view12turn29view11turn30view6turn32view5
- **Protocol thesis**: The decisive shift is from artifact to protocol. On this view, the artwork increasingly resides in a rule-bound specification for producing or selecting outputs. citeturn7search14turn37view4turn31view7turn31view8
- **Distributed authorship thesis**: AI art’s primary novelty is not style or automation, but the exposure of authorship as distributed across artist, training data, model, prompt, reference asset, seed, and platform. citeturn30view3turn32view3turn30view6turn32view10
- **Market-structure thesis**: AI art is also a new market form, because prompts, model constraints, and executable packages can themselves become the object of sale. citeturn31view4turn31view5turn31view6turn31view7turn31view9

### Method and evidentiary stance

Two cautions should govern the paper. First, prehistoric art permits strong description but weak motive attribution; competing explanations should be presented as hypotheses, not facts. Second, claims about crime, laundering, or legal status in the art market must distinguish documented vulnerabilities from headline mythology, and legal authorship from art-historical authorship. citeturn28view2turn30view8turn30view10turn32view10turn30view3

## Comparative matrix and authorship transformation

### Cross-period comparison

| Case | What counts as the work | Main function | Who authors it | What is sold, revered, or preserved |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cave painting | Site-specific marked surface plus ritual setting | Possible ritual, memory, symbolic cognition, group identity | Socially embedded makers; motive uncertain | The site and its images |
| Religious icon | Image-presence within devotional practice | Veneration, mediation, orthodoxy, sacred attention | Workshop under theological rules | The image as devotional interface |
| Renaissance commission | Contracted object for patron and place | Honor, salvation, lineage, prestige | Patron, workshop, and named master together | The commissioned object and its patronal function |
| Dutch market painting | Portable domestic picture | Possession, taste, adornment, collecting, class-marking | Market-facing painter and buyer network | The portable work as property |
| Readymade | Selection plus nomination in art context | Definition-testing, anti-retinal critique, institutional displacement | Artist as selector / declarer | The designated object and its discursive framing |
| Conceptual instruction | Rule set, score, or textual proposition | Delegated realization, participation, dematerialization | Artist as score-writer | The instruction, certificate, and realizations |
| Generative code artwork | System that yields variable outputs from rules and seeds | Delegated form-making and edition variability | Artist-programmer plus code and randomness | The code, edition logic, and minted output |
| AI prompt-score artwork | Executable package: prompt, model, assets, seeds, adapters, rights | Directed exploration of model-conditioned possibility space | Distributed assemblage: artist, prompt, dataset, model, platform, user | Sometimes the image; increasingly the package, license, and realization rights |

The strongest continuity is between conceptual score-based art and AI package-based work. The strongest discontinuity is that AI images emerge from opaque statistical models trained on massive archives rather than from comparatively legible written instructions or deterministic code alone. That makes AI works less like a simple score and more like a score executed inside a historically contingent, model-dependent archive-machine. citeturn28view2turn29view2turn29view4turn29view5turn29view8turn19search1turn19search4turn37view4turn7search14turn29view11turn29view12turn31view7turn31view8turn31view9turn30view6turn32view5turn30view3

### Conceptual table of changing authorship

| Period | Primary author-role | Delegated agent or co-producer | What stabilizes authorship | Variability of output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Prehistoric | Group maker / ritual specialist | Community, site, inherited convention | Archaeological context, style clusters | Low visibility to us; probably varied in use |
| Sacred / imperial | Patron-king, temple, or authorized workshop | Artisans, ritual specialists, court ideology | Function, inscription, dynastic context | Moderate |
| Medieval | Workshop under sacred norms | Scribes, icon painters, devotional tradition | Liturgy, theology, material continuity | Moderate |
| Renaissance | Named master under patronage | Assistants, workshop, patron, contract | Signature, document, patronal record | Moderate |
| Open market modernity | Maker for anonymous buyers | Dealers, publishers, critics | Attribution, collecting history, market records | Moderate to high in prints / editions |
| Conceptual art | Artist as nominator / score-writer | Performer, installer, curator, viewer | Instruction, certificate, institutional framing | High |
| Generative art | Artist-programmer | Code, randomness, seed, minting logic | Source code, edition protocol, platform record | High |

From an academic perspective, AI art does not eliminate authorship. It makes explicit the fact that authorship has long been distributed. What is newly acute is the number of operative layers and the instability introduced by model updates, platform rules, and training-data controversies. The U.S. Copyright Office’s current position reinforces this distinction: prompts alone usually do not supply sufficient human control for authorship, but human selection, arrangement, editing, and incorporation of one’s own material can still ground protectable authorship. That legal answer is narrower than the art-historical one, but it supports a distributed rather than vanished account of authorship. citeturn29view7turn29view12turn30view3turn32view3turn30view5

## Strongest arguments and strongest objections

### Strongest arguments in favor of the main thesis

- **The history itself falsifies the “art equals expression” model.** Egyptian funerary complexes, Mesopotamian royal imagery, Roman imperial portraiture, Byzantine icons, Islamic calligraphy, and South Asian temple production all show art doing theological, dynastic, and ritual work before modern expressivism becomes dominant. citeturn28view3turn28view4turn28view6turn29view2turn29view3turn38search3turn38search7turn38search17
- **Authorship has long been distributed.** Workshop practice, patronage, print culture, performance scores, and generative systems all complicate the modern myth of the solitary maker. AI makes that already-existing distribution more visible and more technically granular. citeturn29view7turn29view8turn37view4turn29view12turn31view7
- **Instruction-based and score-based art provides a strong genealogy for prompting.** Fluxus scores, conceptual instructions, and LeWitt’s programmatic language establish that an artwork may reside in a specification for realization rather than a final handcrafted object. citeturn7search14turn37view4turn37view5
- **Generative art already normalized variable outputs.** Galanter’s definition of generative art and platform practices such as deterministic seed-based editions on fxhash show that collectable artworks can be systems whose realizations vary within constraints. citeturn29view12turn31view7turn31view9turn31view10
- **AI’s real novelty is not “machine-made art” as such, but latent-space navigation over model-internalized archives.** Somaini’s discussion of latent space as a cartography of potential images captures something genuinely new: the artwork may be organized as guided traversal of a model’s possibility field. citeturn32view5turn30view6

### Strongest objections

- **Prehistoric continuity can be overstated.** Because motives behind cave images are uncertain, one must avoid turning prehistory into a blank screen for contemporary theory. citeturn28view2turn28view1
- **Prompting is not the same as scoring.** A musical or Fluxus score typically specifies realizable actions more transparently than a natural-language prompt sent into a proprietary model with unstable weights and hidden filters. citeturn37view4turn30view3turn32view10
- **Legal authorship remains narrower than art-historical authorship.** The Copyright Office’s current view is that prompts alone do not ordinarily make the user the author of the output. That weakens maximalist claims about the creative sufficiency of prompting. citeturn30view3
- **Market categories are unstable.** Prompt marketplaces and AI kits may prove ephemeral or low-status commercial layers rather than durable art-historical forms. The paper should avoid equating current platform affordances with settled categories. citeturn32view8turn31view4turn31view5
- **“Latent space” can become a mystifying metaphor.** Technically, it describes model representations; aesthetically, it can be useful, but it should not be allowed to replace medium-specific and institution-specific analysis. citeturn32view5turn30view6

## Latent score art, commercialization, and AI-native editions

### Is “latent score art” a useful category?

**Qualified yes, but only as a narrow subcategory.** The term is analytically useful when the artwork is not primarily the final output image, video, or sound file, but rather an **executable package** that instructs a model-dependent realization. In that narrow sense, “latent score art” names a work whose identity lies in a constrained method for traversing a learned possibility space. The package may include prompts, negative prompts, reference images, model and version requirements, LoRAs or other adapters, composition rules, seed or seed ranges, output constraints, edition certificates, and user-execution or modification rights. That package behaves like a score, but one addressed to a statistical image engine rather than a human performer alone. citeturn31view4turn31view5turn31view6turn31view7turn31view9turn31view10turn32view5

The term becomes less persuasive in two situations. First, when the work is simply an AI-assisted image print selected by an artist after ordinary iterative prompting; there, “AI art” or “AI-assisted image-making” is clearer. Second, when the package is so underdetermined that the model does most of the compositional labor without a stable envelope of intended results. In such cases the analogy to a score weakens, and the Copyright Office’s skepticism toward prompt-only authorship becomes relevant. citeturn30view3turn32view3

My recommended formulation is therefore: **use “latent score art” only for collector-executed or repeatably executable AI works in which the artist specifies a bounded protocol for generating realizations within a model-conditioned latent or conditioning space.** Use “prompt-score art” when the prompt package is central but the latent-space claim would sound too technical. Use “AI art” for the wider field. citeturn32view5turn30view6turn30view3

Three current market facts matter. First, the global art market remained large but soft in 2024, with Art Basel and UBS estimating $57.5 billion in sales and continued high-end weakness. Second, Christie's reported $1.5 billion in private sales in 2024, showing the continuing importance of opaque, relationship-based channels. Third, Deloitte’s art-finance work now treats art-related wealth management, including art-secured lending, as a core part of the field rather than a marginal add-on. Together, these facts support the thesis that art functions not only as culture but also as infrastructure for wealth, liquidity, and institutional status. citeturn32view7turn30view14turn31view2turn31view1

The laundering and secrecy question requires care. FATF and the U.S. Treasury both identify vulnerabilities tied to privacy traditions, third-party intermediaries, and the concentration of value in high-end transactions, but neither source supports the lazy conclusion that the art market is merely a laundering machine. ### Conclusion

The most defensible answer to the research question is that **AI art is best understood as a reconfiguration of older art functions rather than as the end of art, the end of authorship, or the arrival of an entirely incomparable form**. Across history, art has been ritual technology, political theology, workshop labor, patronal display, commodity, medium of mass reproduction, institutional claim, score, system, and asset. AI does not cancel those functions; it compresses several of them into a single technical-cultural stack. The artist may still be a maker, but is increasingly also a selector, world-builder, art director, taste engine, dataset composer, interface tactician, and protocol designer. For the broad field, “AI art” remains the best umbrella. For the narrower class of works sold as executable prompt / model / seed packages, “latent score art” is a strong and genuinely useful term. The paper should therefore conclude not with rupture versus continuity as a binary, but with **reconfiguration** as the more precise historical judgment. citeturn28view3turn29view2turn29view5turn29view8turn19search1turn7search14turn37view4turn29view11turn29view12turn30view6turn32view5turn30view3turn31view4turn31view5turn31view7
